The best things to do in Brussels

Cutting edge art, comic books, beautiful parks and excellent beer are just some of the best things to do in Brussels

Tuesday 14 October 2025
Share
Written by Sarah Schug
Contributing Writer, Belgium
Contributors: Sally Tipper & Daniela Toporek

Brussels may not always get the spotlight, but beneath its rough exterior lies a city full of character and quirky charm. It might not be as glamorous as its neighbours London and Paris, but look closer and you’ll find a city bursting with personality: Jaw-dropping Art Nouveau architecture, lively markets, vibrant art and food scenes, and laid-back, welcoming locals. From grand squares to tucked-away neighbourhoods, Brussels invites you to explore beyond the usual clichés. Here’s your guide to the city’s must-see sights and hidden gems that prove there’s more to it than meets the eye.

What to do in Brussels
1. Bozar
Things to do
Brussels
Photograph: Shutterstock

What is it? A multi-purpose, Art Deco-inspired Centre for Fine Arts building, built in 1929 by Victor Horta and home to galleries, concert halls, cinemas and more. The name BOZAR comes from shortening the French 'Beaux-Arts', meaning 'fine arts'.


Why we love it: If you do one thing in Brussels, check out the Bozar. The city’s flagship cultural centre is the place for art and exhibitions, but it’s also home to over 200 concerts a year, and shows films from all over the world at its cinema. But even if you don’t fancy any of the events it’s putting on when you visit (impossible), the museum is well worth a visit for the architecture alone, designed by Belgian architect Victor Horta. The best part? You can get in free with a Brussels Card.

Address: Rue Ravensteinstraat 23, 1000, Brussels.

Opening hours: Tuesday-Sunday 10am-6pm, closed Monday (event times vary by programme)

Expect to pay: Prices vary by event

2. Atomium
Things to do
Brussels
Photograph: Shutterstock

What is it? A bizarre, silver atom-shaped structure that was built for the World’s Fair of 1958. It's designed in the form of an iron crystal that has been magnified 165 billion times, and attracts over 800,000 visitors per year.

Why we love it: Belgium prides itself on its quirkiness, and this is unlike any other tourist landmark. Five of its nine spherical rooms host exhibitions on subjects ranging from the history of air travel to the surrealist art of René Magritte, joined by escalators in the narrow connecting tubes (not for the claustrophobic). The surrounding parkland is ideal for picnicking, and the next-door Brussels Design Museum lets you dive into the world of Belgian design. 

Address: Pl. de l'Atomium 1, 1020, Brussels

Opening hours: Open daily from 10am-6pm (last entry at 5.30pm)

Expect to pay: €16 per adult, €14 for seniors over age 65, €8.50 for students and kids from 115cm to age 17, free for kids under 115cm

3. Comic strip murals
Photograph: Shutterstock.com

What is it? A conglomerate of fantastic murals that started popping up in 1991, and are scattered across the city – bringing colour and vibrancy (and Tintin) to the streets of Brussels.

Why we love it: Perfect for your day of strolling – and your Instagram feed – Brussels is filled with huge paintings and murals, most of them in the style of comic book strips. And don’t worry, you won’t miss out on any, as you can download a comic book map of the city, which guides you through all the major street art in the city. Belgium is the land of Tintin, remember, so he features a lot. 

Address: Spot these murals in various locations across the city

Opening hours: Open daily

Expect to pay: Free to the public

4. Grand Place
Photograph: Shutterstock.com

What is it? A colourful, UNESCO-listed cobblestoned square that began in the 11th-century as the town's market square and developed over the centuries. It's surrounded by gilded guildhalls that were once the headquarters of the city’s merchants.

Why we love it: In a city that’s short on must-see landmarks, this is one you really must-see. It’s been burned, bombed and rebuilt over the centuries, but has emerged as magnificent as it ever was. Once you’ve taken in the opulence (and checked out the tiny Manneken Pis just off the square), learn a little about its history in the Brussels City Museum.

Address: Grote Markt, 1000, Brussels

Opening hours: Public square is open 24 hours 

Expect to pay: Visiting the square is free to the public. The nearby Brussels City Museum costs €10 per adult.

5. Rooftop 58
Photograph: Shutterstock

What is it? A massive rooftop terrace made up of two platform levels in the heart of Brussels. It's on top of a former parking garage and offers immaculate views of the city. 

Why we love it: Having just opened in 2023, this isn’t just the largest panoramic rooftop bar in Brussels, but in the whole of Europe. Rooftop 58’s bar, eatery and lofty outdoor space stretch out over 3000 sq m and offer dazzling 360 degree views of the Belgian capital, from the Atomium to the Palace of Justice. Best enjoyed at sunset time with a Basil Smash in hand.

Address: Rue de l’évèque 1, 1000, Brussels

Opening hours: Tuesday-Sunday 5pm-1am, closed Monday

Expect to pay: €6-18 for drinks and €10-30 for small dishes (varies by event and menu)

6. Villa Empain
Photograph: Shutterstock

What is it? A cultural centre and exhibition space in an iconic art deco jewel that used to be a private residence. The building lived many lives, housing embassies, offices, and even went through a rough patch where it was left unused and without proper repair until 2007.

Why we love it: While the Boghossian Foundation uses this unique space to stage temporary art shows, the building alone is worth the entrance fee. The stunning, colonnade-surrounded pool in Villa Empain’s attached garden provides a magical backdrop, but you’ll have to fight that urge to jump in – swimming is unfortunately forbidden. And by the way, if you leave via the back, you find yourself in Brussels’ green lung Bois de la Cambre, a giant park with a couple of cute cafés and a pond where you can rent row boats.

Address: Avenue Franklin Roosevelt, 67, 1050, Brussels

Opening hours: Tuesday-Sunday 11am-6pm, closed Monday

Expect to pay: €12 per adult, €8 for students, teachers, seniors and people with disability

7. Jeu de Balle flea market
Photograph: Shutterstock.com

What is it? A large, outdoor market in the Marolles that's open throughout the week and has been in the same location since 1873. Find around 300 stalls of vintage goods, vinyl, antiques, old photography and more. 

Why we love it: This sprawling collection of vendors has everything you could possibly want, practically. Looking for unique, vintage artwork? They have that. Want kitsch crockery? They've got that too. Even if you're after a wood carving, an armchair and an art deco coffee table – you're sure to find it all here. You just have to spend enough time rummaging around the numerous stalls. Take a break from all the bargaining at quirky restaurant-bar Le Chaff, whose terrace overlooks the hustle bustle of the market. It’s also known for hosting gigs of local bands on Monday nights. 

Address: Place du Jeu de Balle 79, Marolles, 1000, Brussels

Opening hours: Monday-Friday around 9am-2pm, Saturday-Sunday 9am-3pm (individual stall times can vary)

Cost to pay: Market entry is free, but stall prices vary

8. Art Nouveau architecture
Photograph: Shutterstock

What is it? Brussels’ streets are filled with the most beautiful Art Nouveau houses, many built by the style's famous architect, Victor Horta. His construction of the Hôtel Tassel in 1892 led to a new movement in architecture that was loved by the new post-Industrial economic era.

Why we love it: Belgium was home to some of the biggest names in Art Nouveau – the likes of Henry Van de Velde and Victor Horta – and their legacy is everywhere you look. Keep an eye out for the trademark curves and flourishes in wood, metal and stained glass, with ornate mosaics above every other doorway. Notable spots include Horta’s own house, now a museum, and the Belgian Comic Strip Center. Again, the tourist office sells maps for self-guided walking tours.

Address: Find Art Nouveau architecture all across the city

9. Chasseurs Ardennais Market
Photograph: Marché De La Place Des Chasseurs Ardennais

What is it? A Friday evening market in the northern district of Schaerbeek. With so much going on in Brussels, this market is overlooked by tourists, but it's absolutely worth the stop to soak up the residential ambiance and shop local.

Why we love it: This is where the locals gather to welcome in the weekend with a drink and a chat. You’ll find the usual fruit, veg, cheese and deli stalls you’d expect at any market, plus food trucks and mobile wine bars, with cafe patrons spilling out onto the neighbouring terraces on sunny evenings. The Chasseurs Ardennais Market is the sort of thing Brussels excels at; it’s cool, but the vibe is still very neighbourhoody, and you’ll find similar events around the city on other nights.

Address: Pl. des Chasseurs Ardennais 7, 1030, Brussels

Opening hours: Fridays only from 2pm-8pm

Expect to pay: Market entry is free, but stall prices vary

10. Beer tasting tour
Photograph: Shutterstock

What is it? A thirst-quenching beer tour that gives visitors a chance to discover some of the thousands of brews that Belgium is renowned for. Most tours come with snacks, too! 

Why we love it: Don’t know your lambic from your dubbel, your tripel from your trappist, your gueuze from your elbow? You will by the end of this tour, which takes in some lesser-known spots in the city with the aim of educating you about one of Belgium’s finest contributions to the world. Led by a knowledgeable and professional guide, you’ll enjoy several kinds of beer, cheese and some real local colour as you learn.

Address: Starting points vary by tour chosen
