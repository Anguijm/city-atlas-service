# Urban Explorer Research: Taos

Here is a walking guide to the historic heart of Taos, New Mexico, designed for urban exploration off the beaten path.

### **Taos Downtown Historic District**

**The Vibe:** Centered around the historic Taos Plaza, this district is the cultural and spiritual heart of the village. The vibe is earthy and mystical, a slow-paced world of rustic, centuries-old adobe buildings set against the backdrop of the Sangre de Cristo Mountains. It’s a place where Native American, Spanish colonial, and Anglo-American frontier histories converge on narrow, winding streets. Exploring on foot feels like a discovery, with art galleries, hidden courtyards, and the scent of burning piñon wood creating a sensory experience found nowhere else.

---

### **Cafes & Drink Spots**

**1. Adobe Bar at The Historic Taos Inn**
- **What makes it worth visiting:** Known affectionately as "the living room of Taos," this is a genuine gathering place for locals and visitors alike. Set in the lobby of the iconic inn, it’s the perfect spot for people-watching, with a laid-back atmosphere, an impressive tequila selection, and frequent live local music. It’s less a tourist bar and more a community hub.
- **Location:** 125 Paseo Del Pueblo Norte
- **Photo Challenge:** Capture a photo that embodies the "living room" vibe, focusing on a local musician performing or the warm, rustic interior during the evening.
- **Scavenger Hunt:** Find the signature "Cowboy Buddha" margarita on the menu and ask the bartender what makes it a Taos favorite.

**2. Treehouse Bar at Lambert’s of Taos**
- **What makes it worth visiting:** Located upstairs from the main restaurant, the Treehouse Bar offers a more casual, elevated perspective on the historic district. It’s an ideal spot for a well-crafted happy hour cocktail away from the main plaza crowds, housed in a historic building that adds to its intimate, slightly hidden feel.
- **Location:** 123 Bent St
- **Photo Challenge:** From the upstairs perch, capture a "treehouse" perspective of the historic street below, especially as the afternoon light hits the old adobe walls.
- **Scavenger Hunt:** Find a gourmet appetizer on the happy hour menu that uses a distinctly regional New Mexican ingredient.

### **Restaurants & Food Spots**

**1. Orlando’s New Mexican Café**
- **What makes it worth visiting:** A local favorite mentioned by residents, Orlando's is celebrated for its authentic, chile-focused New Mexican food and vibrant, unpretentious atmosphere. The colorful Day of the Dead motif and the cozy patio, heated by a wood-burning fire ring in cooler weather, make it a memorable and distinctly Taos experience.
- **Location:** 1114 Don Juan Valdez Ln
- **Photo Challenge:** At dusk, photograph the patio with the fire ring glowing, capturing the colorful, festive decor against the twilight sky.
- **Scavenger Hunt:** Locate the most unique or elaborate calavera (decorated skull) in the restaurant's decor.

**2. Michael’s Kitchen Restaurant and Bakery**
- **What makes it worth visiting:** This is where Taos locals go to catch up. A beloved institution since 1974, Michael's serves classic New Mexican breakfasts like huevos rancheros and is famous for its fresh-made pastries, including hubcap-sized cinnamon rolls. The walls are covered in local art, making it feel like a true community crossroads.
- **Location:** 304 Paseo Del Pueblo Norte
- **Photo Challenge:** Get a creative, forced-perspective shot with one of the giant cinnamon rolls, making it look as big as a person's head.
- **Scavenger Hunt:** Find a piece of art for sale on the walls painted by a local artist whose name you can find.

**3. The Love Apple**
- **What makes it worth visiting:** For a truly unique Taos meal, The Love Apple serves regional New Mexican dishes in what was once a 100-year-old adobe chapel. The setting is intimate and quirky, and the menu is dedicated to seasonal, locally sourced ingredients, featuring dishes like rainbow trout wrapped in corn husks. It’s a cash-only spot that perfectly captures the town's spirit.
- **Location:** 803 Paseo Del Pueblo Norte
- **Photo Challenge:** Capture the exterior of the old adobe chapel at night, focusing on the contrast between the historic, sacred architecture and the warm, inviting light from within.
- **Scavenger Hunt:** Identify an ingredient on the current menu that is explicitly sourced from a local farm or ranch.

### **Other Interesting Spots**

**1. Our Lady of Guadalupe Parish**
- **What makes it worth visiting:** Just west of the main plaza, this beautiful and historic adobe church is an active parish and a cornerstone of the community. Its serene presence and classic New Mexican religious architecture offer a peaceful and authentic alternative to the more heavily touristed mission church south of town.
- **Location:** 205 Don Fernando St
- **Photo Challenge:** Frame a shot of the church's simple, elegant adobe bell towers against the deep blue New Mexico sky, ideally in the morning light.
- **Scavenger Hunt:** Find the oldest-looking hand-carved wooden feature on the church's exterior (like a door or window frame).

**2. Kit Carson House**
- **What makes it worth visiting:** A short walk east of the plaza, this historic adobe home was once owned by the legendary and controversial frontiersman, Kit Carson. The building itself is a preserved piece of 19th-century Taos, offering a tangible link to the Old West history that shaped the town, separate from the main museum circuit.
- **Location:** 113 Kit Carson Rd
- **Photo Challenge:** Take a photo that juxtaposes the building's historic, mud-plastered adobe wall with a modern element on the street, like a passing car or contemporary sign.
- **Scavenger Hunt:** Locate the official historical marker or plaque on the property and find the exact years Kit Carson lived in the house.

**3. Governor Charles Bent House**
- **What makes it worth visiting:** This historic site was the home of Charles Bent, the first American governor of New Mexico, who was assassinated here during the Taos Revolt of 1847. The museum offers a raw, unfiltered glimpse into the violent and complex history of the American West's expansion. The bullet holes are still visible.
- **Location:** 117 Bent St
- **Photo Challenge:** Focus on an architectural detail, like a weathered wooden *viga* (ceiling beam) or a deep-set window, that speaks to the building's age and history.
- **Scavenger Hunt:** Find the "escape hole" through which the family fled during the revolt, a powerful and tangible piece of the house's dramatic story.
